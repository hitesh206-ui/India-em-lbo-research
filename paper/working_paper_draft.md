Leveraged Buyouts in Emerging Markets:

Capital Structure Adaptation, Regulatory Frictions, and the Case of India

Working Paper Draft

Hitesh Dabi

Independent Researcher, Jaipur, India

Himanshu Dabi

Independent Researcher, Jaipur, India

May 2026

Abstract: This paper develops a framework for understanding leveraged buyouts in emerging markets, using India as the central case. The conventional leveraged buyout model, developed primarily in the United States and Western Europe, assumes deep acquisition-finance markets, predictable legal enforcement, creditor coordination, and the ability to place substantial debt at or around the acquisition vehicle and operating company. These assumptions do not transfer cleanly to India. Indian buyouts are shaped by financial-assistance restrictions, takeover rules, foreign borrowing constraints, local debt-market depth, promoter and family ownership, insolvency timing, and sector-specific regulatory frictions. The paper argues that Indian buyouts are not merely underleveraged versions of US LBOs; rather, they are adapted buyout structures in which private equity sponsors rely more heavily on growth, governance, structured credit, hybrid instruments, and exit-route planning than on high acquisition leverage. The paper contributes a conceptual model for India-adapted LBO analysis, proposes a regulatory-friction scoring framework, and outlines a replicable data and modeling approach for future empirical work. The intended contribution is practical as well as academic: the framework can help researchers, private equity professionals, private credit investors, and finance students distinguish between the mechanics of developed-market LBOs and the institutional realities of emerging-market buyouts.

Keywords: leveraged buyouts; private equity; emerging markets; India; capital structure; private credit; acquisition finance; regulatory frictions; corporate governance

# 1. Introduction

Leveraged buyouts are often taught through a developed-market template: a private equity sponsor acquires a target using a meaningful amount of debt, improves operations and governance during a multi-year holding period, repays or refinances debt from cash flows and exit proceeds, and seeks to realize an attractive equity return through sale or public-market exit. This template is analytically useful because it forces discipline around cash generation, debt capacity, capital allocation, and exit valuation. It is also incomplete when transported into emerging markets.

India provides a particularly useful setting for examining the limits of the standard LBO model. It has a large and growing private capital ecosystem, a deep public equity market, expanding domestic institutional capital, and an increasingly active private credit market. At the same time, India does not have the same acquisition-finance architecture that historically supported large US and European LBOs. Buyouts must operate within legal constraints on financial assistance, takeover regulations for listed companies, foreign-exchange and borrowing rules, domestic lender risk appetite, promoter ownership dynamics, and the timing uncertainty of restructuring and exit routes.

The central argument of this paper is that Indian LBOs should not be assessed as failed or incomplete versions of US LBOs. They should be understood as adapted buyout structures. In these structures, sponsors may use lower acquisition leverage, rely more on equity and co-investment capital, employ non-convertible debentures, compulsorily convertible debentures, structured private credit, or INR term loans, and place greater emphasis on operating growth, governance improvement, and exit optionality. The economic logic remains recognizably related to private equity: ownership concentration, active governance, management incentives, and value creation. But the financing architecture differs.

This paper is deliberately positioned as a working paper. Its purpose is to develop a conceptual and replicable framework that can later support empirical testing. The paper proposes research questions, hypotheses, a data architecture, a regulatory-friction score, and a model design that compares a standard US-style LBO with an India-adapted LBO. It also identifies the areas where future data collection is required, especially transaction-level leverage, instrument choice, borrowing cost, and exit-route information.

# 2. Research Questions and Contributions

The paper is organized around five research questions. First, why are classic US-style LBOs less common in India? Second, how do Indian buyout capital structures differ from developed-market LBO structures? Third, what role do local instruments such as non-convertible debentures, compulsorily convertible debentures, INR term loans, and private credit play in filling acquisition-finance gaps? Fourth, do legal and regulatory frictions materially reduce feasible leverage or alter the location of debt within the group structure? Fifth, how should an analyst modify a standard LBO model when applying it to Indian or emerging-market transactions?

The paper contributes in three ways. The first contribution is conceptual. It reframes Indian LBOs as adapted buyout structures rather than inferior versions of developed-market LBOs. This distinction matters because a direct comparison based only on debt-to-EBITDA may understate the relevance of governance, growth, and structured finance in emerging-market private equity.

The second contribution is methodological. The paper proposes a regulatory-friction scoring framework that can be paired with transaction-level capital-structure data. The framework is intended to convert qualitative legal and institutional frictions into a transparent, auditable score. This allows a researcher to test whether higher-friction jurisdictions or transaction types are associated with lower leverage, higher equity contribution, more hybrid instruments, or different exit routes.

The third contribution is practical. The paper outlines an India-adapted LBO model that differs from the standard Wall Street model in the treatment of debt instruments, interest cost, currency, taxes, promoter ownership, financing location, and exit probability. The model is intended to be useful for students and analysts who understand the mechanics of LBO modeling but need to adapt those mechanics to an Indian setting.

# 3. Literature Review

The modern LBO literature generally emphasizes leverage, governance, incentives, and operational improvement. Kaplan and Stromberg describe leveraged buyouts and private equity as transactions in which specialized investment firms acquire companies using a combination of sponsor equity and outside debt, while also reshaping governance and incentives. This broad framing is useful because it avoids reducing private equity to leverage alone.

Jensen's agency-cost logic remains central to the intellectual foundation of LBOs. High leverage can reduce managerial discretion by committing free cash flow to debt service, while concentrated ownership and performance incentives can align managers with owners. In developed markets, this theory helps explain why LBOs can create value even when financial leverage is risky. However, the theory assumes that leverage can be imposed, monitored, refinanced, and enforced within a sufficiently reliable institutional environment.

Axelson, Jenkinson, Stromberg, and Weisbach show that leverage and pricing in buyouts are strongly related to credit-market conditions rather than simply to the leverage of comparable public companies. This finding is especially relevant for emerging markets. If the availability and cost of credit drive LBO capital structures in developed markets, then the shallower, more fragmented, or more regulated credit markets of emerging economies should have a direct effect on leverage, instrument choice, and pricing.

Emerging-market private equity literature adds a second dimension: institutional frictions may create both risks and opportunities. Cole, Melecky, Molders, and Reed examine long-run private equity returns in emerging and frontier markets and link performance to financial frictions, economic growth, banking-system development, and capital controls. Their findings are consistent with the possibility that private equity in emerging markets operates differently not only because markets are less mature, but because the source of value creation may be tied to overcoming frictions that developed-market investors do not face in the same form.

The India-specific literature is less developed with respect to LBOs. Practitioner reports document the scale and evolution of India's private equity and venture capital market, while recent private credit reports point to the growing importance of non-bank capital. Yet the academic literature has not fully integrated India's legal, regulatory, promoter-ownership, and credit-market constraints into a unified LBO framework. This paper attempts to fill that gap by proposing a framework that can later be tested with transaction data.

# 4. Institutional Background: Why India Is Different

India's institutional environment differs from the US-style LBO setting in several ways. First, ownership structure matters. Many Indian companies, including listed companies, are controlled by promoter families or founder groups. A sponsor seeking control may need to negotiate not only valuation and financing, but also promoter rollover, family governance, management continuity, succession concerns, and public shareholder protection. This changes both the transaction process and the post-acquisition governance model.

Second, Indian law restricts financial assistance by public companies for the purchase or subscription of their own shares or those of their holding company. Section 67 of the Companies Act, 2013 is therefore central to the discussion of acquisition finance. In a classic LBO, lenders often expect the operating company's assets and cash flows to support acquisition debt. In India, the legal ability to push acquisition debt onto the target or use target support for acquisition financing may be constrained, especially for public companies. The result is a greater need for careful holding-company structuring, sponsor-level equity, bridge financing, or alternative instruments.

Third, listed-company acquisitions are affected by the SEBI Substantial Acquisition of Shares and Takeovers Regulations, 2011. These rules shape the acquisition of substantial shareholding and control, including disclosure and open-offer obligations. For LBO analysis, the key point is not merely compliance cost but deal design: timing, offer size, financing certainty, public shareholder participation, and exit strategy can all be affected by takeover regulation.

Fourth, foreign borrowing and currency regulation affect the feasibility of offshore leverage. India's external commercial borrowing framework and related foreign-exchange rules shape who may borrow externally, from whom, at what maturity, at what cost, and for what end use. For a sponsor accustomed to dollar or euro leveraged loans, these constraints can materially alter the economics of an India acquisition. If acquisition debt cannot be raised offshore on the same terms or cannot be applied in the same manner, local currency debt or structured credit may become more important.

Fifth, creditor enforcement and restructuring timing affect debt capacity. The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code has improved India's insolvency architecture, but investors still model timing, recoveries, litigation risk, and enforcement uncertainty. From an LBO perspective, lenders will price not only expected cash flows but also the enforceability of collateral and the expected path of resolution in downside cases.

Sixth, exit routes matter. In developed markets, a sponsor may assume several credible exit routes: sale to a strategic acquirer, sale to another sponsor, dividend recapitalization, or IPO. In India, public equity markets can be deep and receptive, but exits may still be influenced by market windows, sector appetite, promoter arrangements, regulatory approvals, and minimum public shareholding requirements. These factors shift the return model away from a purely debt-paydown story and toward a broader value-creation and exit-readiness strategy.

# 5. Hypotheses

This paper advances four testable hypotheses. H1: Indian and emerging-market buyouts use lower acquisition leverage than comparable US and European LBOs. The rationale is that higher borrowing costs, legal restrictions, limited acquisition-finance depth, and enforcement risk reduce the level of debt that can be prudently placed on a transaction.

H2: Indian buyouts rely more heavily on hybrid and structured instruments than classic developed-market LBOs. These instruments may include non-convertible debentures, compulsorily convertible debentures, structured private credit, mezzanine-style instruments, sponsor loans, and INR-denominated term debt. The expected role of these instruments is to bridge the gap between pure equity and conventional senior acquisition debt.

H3: Regulatory friction reduces feasible leverage and increases required sponsor equity contribution. If the target cannot provide direct or indirect support for acquisition debt, or if foreign borrowing and takeover rules raise execution risk, a sponsor must either reduce debt, move debt to a different entity, increase equity, or use instruments with different legal and economic characteristics.

H4: Growth and operational value creation are relatively more important in India-adapted LBOs than in high-leverage developed-market LBOs. When entry leverage is lower and debt amortization contributes less to equity returns, revenue growth, margin expansion, governance improvement, working-capital discipline, and exit multiple management become more important drivers of MOIC and IRR.

# 6. Conceptual Framework: Standard LBO vs. India-Adapted LBO

A standard US-style LBO model begins with enterprise value, sponsor equity, debt tranches, transaction fees, operating projections, cash sweep, exit multiple, and equity-return analysis. The central return mechanics are entry valuation, leverage, cash-flow-driven debt repayment, EBITDA growth, and exit valuation. In a simplified model, debt amplifies equity returns if the company generates sufficient cash flow and exits at a reasonable valuation.

An India-adapted LBO model must preserve the core mechanics while changing several assumptions. The first change is debt capacity. Instead of assuming a developed-market leveraged loan package, the model should begin with a conservative debt-capacity analysis tied to local borrowing cost, collateral, cash-flow stability, legal constraints, and refinancing availability. The second change is instrument mapping. Debt should be split into instruments such as INR term loans, NCDs, CCDs, private credit, vendor financing, or sponsor loans, each with different cost, maturity, conversion, security, and cash-flow implications.

The third change is the location of debt. A developed-market model may implicitly assume that acquisition debt can be pushed down to the operating company. In India, an analyst should explicitly identify whether debt is located at the acquisition vehicle, holding company, operating company, promoter entity, or another group entity, and whether cash can legally and practically move to service it. The fourth change is regulatory friction. The model should include a qualitative-to-quantitative adjustment that reduces debt capacity, increases transaction cost, delays closing, or changes exit probability.

The fifth change is exit modeling. Instead of assuming a single exit multiple, the India-adapted model should use probability-weighted exit routes. An IPO exit may have a different valuation, timing, and liquidity profile than a strategic sale or secondary sponsor sale. The model should therefore show not only base-case IRR, but also downside, delayed-exit, refinancing, and lower-multiple scenarios.

# 7. Proposed Regulatory-Friction Score

The regulatory-friction score is intended to make institutional analysis transparent. It does not claim legal precision; rather, it provides a structured way to compare transactions or jurisdictions. A score can be assigned on a 1-to-5 scale, where 1 indicates low friction and 5 indicates high friction. Each category can be weighted based on expected relevance to the transaction.

The proposed categories are: financial-assistance constraints, takeover and public-shareholder rules, foreign borrowing and currency rules, tax leakage and deductibility, insolvency and enforcement risk, sector-specific approvals, promoter or related-party complexity, and exit-route constraints. A transaction involving a private target with stable cash flows, domestic financing, and no sector approval may receive a lower score. A public-company acquisition requiring an open offer, complex promoter rollover, offshore borrowing, and sector approvals may receive a higher score.

The score can then be connected to model assumptions. For example, a higher score may reduce maximum debt-to-EBITDA, increase transaction fees, widen interest margins, extend closing timelines, reduce exit probability, or increase required equity. The value of the framework is not that the score is perfect; it is that the assumptions become explicit and auditable.

# 8. Data and Empirical Strategy

The proposed empirical strategy begins with a hand-collected dataset of Indian and emerging-market buyout or control transactions. Each observation should include target name, sector, country, sponsor, announcement date, transaction type, enterprise value, EBITDA where available, debt amount, equity contribution, instrument type, interest cost, exit route, and public source. The dataset should separately identify full buyouts, control investments, take-private transactions, platform acquisitions, minority growth investments, and structured credit transactions, because these categories have different economic meanings.

The empirical analysis should proceed in four stages. First, describe the sample by year, sector, sponsor type, transaction type, and exit route. Second, compare capital structures across India, other emerging markets, and developed-market benchmarks. Third, estimate whether regulatory-friction scores are associated with lower leverage, greater hybrid instrument usage, or higher equity contribution. Fourth, run model-based sensitivity analysis to show how different leverage and exit assumptions affect sponsor returns.

A key limitation is data availability. Indian transaction documents often do not disclose complete financing terms. Public news sources may report equity value but not enterprise value, or investor name but not debt instrument. This limitation should be addressed transparently by coding missing values, triangulating sources, and distinguishing verified data from estimated assumptions. A public GitHub repository can improve research credibility by making the data template, cleaning code, assumptions log, and model outputs available for replication.

# 9. Model Design for the India-Adapted LBO

The model should contain two parallel cases: a standard developed-market LBO and an India-adapted LBO. Both cases should begin with the same operating company, revenue, EBITDA, capital expenditure, working capital, tax rate, and exit assumptions. The difference should be in the financing structure, borrowing cost, debt location, regulatory friction, and exit probability.

The standard case may assume a senior secured term loan, junior or mezzanine debt, cash sweep, and a conventional exit multiple. The India-adapted case should assume lower initial leverage, higher interest cost, possible use of NCDs or private credit, limited cash sweep if legal or structural constraints restrict cash movement, and greater reliance on EBITDA growth. The model should separately show operating value creation and financial-structure value creation.

The output should include sources and uses, debt schedule, interest schedule, free cash flow, debt paydown, exit enterprise value, exit equity value, MOIC, IRR, and sensitivity tables. Sensitivities should include entry multiple, exit multiple, revenue CAGR, EBITDA margin, debt-to-EBITDA, interest cost, holding period, tax rate, and regulatory-friction score. This design allows the analyst to demonstrate why a lower-leverage India deal may still be attractive if growth and governance improvements are strong.

# 10. Discussion: What Makes an Indian Buyout Investable?

The investability of an Indian buyout depends less on whether it matches a US leverage template and more on whether the sponsor can solve three problems: control, cash flow, and exit. Control refers to the ability to influence governance, management incentives, capital allocation, and strategic direction. In promoter-led companies, control may require negotiated arrangements rather than a simple acquisition of a majority stake. Cash flow refers to the ability of the business to support debt, reinvestment, and distributions under Indian legal and tax constraints. Exit refers to credible paths for liquidity, including IPOs, strategic sales, secondary sponsor sales, or structured sell-downs.

This framing also changes how private credit should be viewed. In a US-style LBO, private credit may be one of several debt providers competing to finance an acquisition. In India, private credit may be a more central part of the buyout architecture because it can offer flexible structures when bank acquisition finance is limited or when transaction features do not fit conventional lending templates. However, private credit is not a free substitute for bank debt. It often comes with higher cost, tighter covenants, stronger collateral expectations, and more bespoke negotiation.

The practical implication is that the India-adapted LBO analyst should not begin with the question, 'How much leverage can we force into this transaction?' The better question is, 'Which capital structure best matches the legal constraints, cash-flow profile, governance plan, and exit strategy of this company?' This is a more conservative question, but also a more realistic one.

# 11. Limitations and Future Research

This paper has several limitations. First, it is conceptual and framework-building rather than a completed empirical study. The hypotheses require transaction-level data that must be collected and verified. Second, Indian buyout financing details are often not publicly available, which may create sample-selection and measurement problems. Third, legal and regulatory interpretation changes over time and must be verified with current primary sources before being applied to live transactions.

Future research can extend the paper in several directions. A first extension would build a deal-level database of Indian control transactions and compare them with developed-market LBO benchmarks. A second extension would examine the role of private credit and hybrid instruments in sponsor returns. A third extension would conduct case studies of successful and unsuccessful Indian buyouts, separating operational value creation from leverage-driven returns. A fourth extension would compare India with Brazil, Southeast Asia, South Africa, or China to determine whether the India-adapted model is part of a broader emerging-market buyout pattern.

# 12. Conclusion

This paper argues that Indian LBOs should be analyzed as adapted buyout structures rather than incomplete versions of US LBOs. The standard LBO model remains useful, but its assumptions must be adjusted for India's legal, regulatory, credit-market, ownership, and exit-route environment. In such a setting, leverage remains important but is not the sole or even always the dominant driver of returns. Growth, governance, structured credit, promoter negotiation, and exit planning become central to the buyout thesis.

The broader implication is that emerging-market private equity requires a different capital-structure playbook. Analysts should adapt the model before judging the market. A low-leverage transaction may still be a true buyout if it transfers control, improves governance, increases enterprise value, and produces attractive risk-adjusted returns. Conversely, a highly levered structure may be inappropriate if the legal and institutional environment cannot support it. India therefore offers an important laboratory for rethinking how LBO theory should be applied beyond developed markets.
